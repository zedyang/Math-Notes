\section*{Problem 1}
Prove by contradiction: suppose the covered interest parity does not hold, that is: $F_t/S_t \ne (1+r_f^*)/(1+r_f)$. Consider the following two investment strategies, for simplicity denote \$ as domestic currency and \euro~ as foreign currency.
\begin{itemize}
  \item[1.] Starting with 1\$ at time $t$. Enter \euro ~forward contract at time $t$, save 1\$ in domestic bank (which becomes $(1+r_f)$ \$ at $t+1$), and purchase $(1+r_f)F_t$ \euro ~at $t+1$.
  \item[2.] Starting with 1\$ at time $t$, purchase $S_t$ \euro~ at time $t$, then save $S_t$ \euro~in foreign bank, obtain $(1+r^*_f)S_t$ \euro~at $t+1$.
\end{itemize}
WLOG suppose $\frac{F_t}{S_t} > \frac{1+r_f^*}{1+r_f}$, then one can attain an arbitrage by longing strategy 1 and shorting strategy 2. Since both strategy cost 1\$ at time $t$, the composite arbitrage portfolio has cost zero, and has riskless profit:
$$
(1+r_f)F_t - (1+r^*_f)S_t > 0
$$
If $\frac{F_t}{S_t} < \frac{1+r_f^*}{1+r_f}$ we can do the reverse. Therefore, the assumption must not hold, i.e. $\frac{F_t}{S_t} > \frac{1+r_f^*}{1+r_f}$ must hold to ensure no-arbitrage. $\square$

    So the average return of portfolios \(k\) can be explained as: \[

    did a good job in explaining portfolio returns.
